Introduction

Welcome to our website!!! The topic that you will be exploring while navigating this website is a subject that is not spoken about although it is very important. A lifestyle is the way a person lives, and it can be associated with everything you do, whether it is the way you wash your clothes or the way you travel to school. In this website we will be investigating two polar lifestyles, a pro-active lifestyle and a sedentary lifestyle, further elaborating on why lifestyles are so important. A lifestyle is something everyone in the world has, so this is an important matter that will affect everyone, it will determine a person’s physical and mental health.

Sedentary lifestyle: An increasingly widespread lifestyle stereotyped by their tendency to not reach the recommended 150 minutes of weekly exercise. This category of people pursues little to no physical activity throughout their daily lives. Studies show that a prolonged sedentary lifestyle can increase chances of obesity, type 2 diabetes, types of cancer, cardiovascular disease and possibility of an early death.

A Sedentary/Unhealthy Lifestyle Includes

  • Staying in a stationary position for most of the hours that a person is awake, this could be due to work, school or just generally for entertainment purposes (gaming, binging tv-shows, hobbies)
  • This lifestyle is also associated with those who consume unhealthy food, these foods are most commonly referred to as “junk food”
  • Most importantly not participating in any physical activity, whether this would be going out to exercise or just not being active/not moving in general
  • An addiction to smoking, consumption of alcohol, drugs, etc

Evaluation of your findings What did the research reveal research into the lifestyle choices and habits, widened our perspective of health related statistics based off the sample size of 66 individuals. When we compared the values of exercise done weekly vs sitting down on a spectrum of 0-20+ hours, an interesting trend could be visualised. ”Exercise Weekly” was positively skewed, whereas “Hours Sitting Down” had a symmetrical distribution. This particular finding allows us to conclude, based off the data collected that weekly exercise averages 4-5 hours. Sitting down daily is 8-9 hours. This ratio of 59.5:4.5 “Weekly Sitting Down” to ”Weekly Exercise” respectively infers the percentages 8.3% and 91.7%. This vital piece of data tells us that averagely, people tend to sit down 10 times the amount they exercise. These values were expected as they correlate to the world health organisation guidelines, which recommend around 4 hours of moderate intensity exercise weekly. Within our second comparison graph, "Average weights of boys and girls surveyed (16)", data was filtered for those who had inputed their age as 16. No other ages were investigated due to small sample sizes. Upon inspection of the two line graphs, it is immediately clear that the female weights were more positively skewed, whilst males are negatively skewed. This outcome is reasonable and expected; according to global averages, males generally weigh 12% more than females. Similarly, this secondary source data of global averages, can allow us to determine the health of the individuals surveyed. The results determined for males were unexpected as there was a 15% greater average weight than globally recognised. When looked deeper, This may be directly correlated to another detail found from the survey: “Healthy Eating”. Only 49% of respondents rated their diet as healthy, deducing that the other 51% possibly make bad dietary decisions, ultimately leading to larger weights Lastly we analysed what people did most while sitting down. 55% said they were studying, 6% worked, 22% Spent their time on entertainment and 17% other. The pie chart depicts that studying was the dominant category. Upon further investigation, this particular statistic can be explained by the limitation of data collation mostly only coming from classmates. This caused bias toward the specific age group, 16 year olds. This possible fault in data collection led to similarly biased results that mostly reflected a lifestyle of students in senior school. Ultimately, this justifies why work was the least common activity when sitting down, whilst studying is the highest.
